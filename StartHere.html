Factorization in 2D: Explanation + Export Tools
Notation & What We Are Plotting

Every integer $n \geq 2$ has a unique factorization into prime powers. We write it keeping track of the distinct primes and their exponents:

$$n = \prod_{k=1}^{m_n} d_k^{e_k}$$
SymbolMeaning
$d_k$Distinct prime factors of $n$, sorted ascending
$e_k$Exponent of $d_k$ in the factorization
$m_n$Number of distinct prime factors — little omega $\omega(n)$
$M_n = \displaystyle\sum e_k$Total prime factors counting multiplicity — big omega $\Omega(n)$

Example — $n = 60 = 2^2 \cdot 3^1 \cdot 5^1$: $\{d_k\}=\{2,3,5\}$, $\{e_k\}=\{2,1,1\}$, $m_{60}=3$, $M_{60}=4$.

We pick a period $T_o \in \mathbb{Q}$ and define the primitive phase unit:

$$\omega = e^{2\pi i / T_o}$$

Each prime factor $d_k$ is mapped to a direction on the unit circle via $\omega^{d_k}$. Only $d_k \bmod T_o$ matters for the angle, so small integer $T_o$ collapses many primes to the same few directions.

The three sequences
SequenceDefinitionDescription
$s_n$ $\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{m_n} e_k\, \omega^{d_k}$ Each prime $d_k$ contributes once per multiplicity — if $d_k$ appears $e_k$ times it adds $e_k$ copies of $\omega^{d_k}$
$\dot{s}_n$ $\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{m_n} \omega^{d_k}$ Each distinct prime contributes exactly one unit vector, regardless of how many times it appears
$\ddot{s}_n$ $\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{m_n} \omega^{e_k^{d_k}}$ Each distinct prime contributes one unit vector at angle index $d_k^{e_k}$ — encoding both the prime and its exponent together

Example — $n = 60$:

$$s_{60} = 2\,\omega^{2} + \omega^3 + \omega^5 \qquad \dot{s}_{60} = \omega^2 + \omega^3 + \omega^5 \qquad \ddot{s}_{60} = \omega^{4} + \omega^3 + \omega^5$$

Primes are always unit vectors: for any prime $p$, $s_p = \dot{s}_p = \omega^p$, so $|s_p|=1$. All primes land on the unit circle — they are sieved out naturally by their modulus.

The 2D scatter plot

Each $n$ is placed at $\bigl(\operatorname{Re}(s_n),\, \operatorname{Im}(s_n)\bigr)$. Color encodes $n \bmod M_c$.

$T_o = 10$ — only the last decimal digit of $p$ matters. Primes (except 2 and 5) end in 1, 3, 7, 9. The geometry is shaped by the golden ratio $\varphi = \frac{1+\sqrt{5}}{2}$, since $\cos(36°) = \varphi/2$.

$T_o = 6$ — primes (except 2 and 3) are always $\equiv 1$ or $5 \pmod{6}$, landing at $\pm 60°$. The geometry is hexagonal, shaped by $\sqrt{3}$.

For large $T_o$ the angles spread continuously and the scatter becomes a dense cloud rather than a discrete lattice.

The 3D plot — choosing the $z$-axis

A third coordinate $w_n$ lifts each point out of the plane. Natural choices:

$w_n$Meaning
$w_n = n$The "time" axis — how the pattern grows as we count
$w_n = M_n$The "mass" axis — total prime factors with multiplicity
$w_n = m_n$The "diversity" axis — number of distinct prime factors
The coupon-collector question

As we count $n = 2, 3, \ldots, N$, how many distinct positions in the complex plane do we visit? Imagine drawing numbers out of a box: before factorizing $n$ we ask — will it land on a position we have already seen, or will it need a new one? The occupancy probability tracks, as a running fraction, what proportion of all $n$ so far landed on a previously visited position.

  • Small integer $T_o$: few possible angles → positions reused very quickly.
  • Large or irrational $T_o$: new positions keep appearing for longer before repetitions dominate.
